II.Core Knowledge ＋ Worked Examples
Knowledge point 1: Establishing equations from chart data 🔴 SSPA Core
Four-step method from charts to equations:
❶ Read charts |||SEP|||: Extract known values ​​(numbers + units) from bar graphs/data tablesSet unknowns |||SEP|||: Set required (or missing) quantities into variables (such as x, y, n)
❷ Find equivalence relationships: "Total", "difference", "several times" and "average" in the question → Convert into equations
❸ List of equations: Write a complete equation using known numbers + variables, ensuring that both sides of the equal sign have equal meanings
❹ series of equations: Write a complete equation using known numbers + variables, ensuring that both sides of the equal sign have equal meanings
🪴 Trap detonation example (the most important demonstration in this class)
According to the reference chart: It is known that the borrowing volume of library A in January is 115 books for novels and 90 books for non-fiction. If the total number of books borrowed by Library A in January is 20 more than the total number of books borrowed by Library B, what is the total number of books borrowed by Library B in January? Assume parallel equations with unknown variables.
❌ Common mistakes (reversed direction of equation)
Suppose the total quantity of library B = y
y + 20 = 115 + 90
→ y = 185 (error: this is the total amount of Hall A)
"A has 20 more than B" was mistakenly written as "y + 20 = A". It should be "A = y + 20" or "A − y = 20"
✅ Correct solution
The total volume of Hall A in January = 115 + 90 = 205
Let the total volume of library B in one month = y
205 − y = 20  →  y = 185
Or: 205 = y + 20 → y = 185
"A has 20 more than B" → A − B = 20 → 205 − y = 20
🧠 Tips: "Read the graph to get the numbers, set up the unknowns, find relationships and write equations, solve the equations to check the answers, and bring back the original questions to check the units."
⚠️ The most frequent error: "A is more than B" is correct to write "B + n = A", but many students write "A + n = B" instead!
⚠️ The second most frequent error: looking at the wrong bar (without looking at the legend) when taking numbers from the chart, resulting in the known numbers in the equation being wrong from the beginning!
